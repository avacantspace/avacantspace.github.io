Understanding Russian Sentence Structure

The English word order tends to be strictly SVO. A sentence with this structure would be phrased with a Subject (S), then a Verb (V) and if there is one, the Object (O) to finish the sentence. There are alternative sentence structures, and SVO is only used by approximately 42% of world languages. The most common word structure is SOV, used by approximately 45%. Just like English, Russian is a SVO language, usually. However there are many more options with word order in Russian. A Russian speaker can choose to place words in different orders depending on the cases being used on the words. If you want to say "Today, the dog caught the cat." you can say "сегодна собака поймала кота" in the same order as English, or you could say "кота поймала цобака сегоня" (cat caught dog today) both of these are saying that the dog caught the cat today perfectly correctly. Russian has six cases and each gives certain information about the function of the word in a sentence. The six different types of cases are:
именительный цадеж - nominative case
роднтельный цадеж - genitive case
дательный цадеж - dative case
винительный цадеж - accusative case
творительный цадеж - intstrumental case
предложный цадеж - prepositial case
Meaning, in Russian you have to pay more attention to the ending of words rather than the structure. So, if you wanted to say "Today the cat caught the dog" you could say "сегодня кот поймал собаку" (the cat caught the dog today).
